Gregory Backpacks Review: Is the Premium Suspension Worth the Price?

In the world of load-hauling, the Gregory Baltoro series has maintained a cult-like following for over a decade. While ultralight hikers might scoff at the base weight of these packs, anyone carrying camera gear, winter equipment, or extra water knows that a good suspension system is far more important than saving a few ounces on fabric.

I took the latest generation of the Gregory Baltoro out for a 7-day backcountry photography trip. With my total pack weight hovering around 45 pounds, I wanted to see if their Response A3 suspension system actually delivers on its promises.

The Response A3 Suspension System

The core of Gregory's premium pricing lies in the Response A3 (Auto Angle Adjust) system. Unlike rigid frames that force your body to adapt to the pack, the shoulder harnesses and hip belts on the Baltoro pivot independently. When you step up onto a high boulder, the hip belt articulates with your movement. The result is a dramatic reduction in the side-to-side sway that typically exhausts your core muscles over a long day.

Field Note: The pivoting hip belt makes a massive difference on uneven terrain. I found myself having to readjust my straps far less frequently than I do with static frame packs.

Organization and Access

Gregory understands that living out of a backpack requires intelligent organization. The U-zip main compartment access means you can lay the pack flat and open it like a duffel bag. This is a game-changer for photographers who need to access lenses buried in the middle of their load without pulling out their sleeping bag and tent first.

The Final Verdict

Is the Gregory Baltoro worth the premium price tag? If your total pack weight rarely exceeds 25 pounds, this pack is overkill. However, if you are a photographer, a winter camper, or a guide who regularly carries 40 to 60 pounds, the Baltoro is an investment in your physical longevity. The suspension system effectively tricks your body into thinking you are carrying less weight than you actually are.
