The Perfect Fit: How to Adjust Your Pack Suspension

I’m Xander Hayes. In my years on the trail, the most common injury I see isn't a twisted ankle—it's agonizing lower back pain and shoulder chafing caused by poorly adjusted backpacks.

Even the most expensive Gregory or Osprey pack will perform like a cheap bag if you don't follow the Correct Tensioning Sequence. Here is my field-tested 4-step guide to achieving the "weightless" carry.

Step 1: The Hip Belt (The Foundation)

Put the pack on. Find your iliac crest (the top of your hip bone). The padded part of the hip belt should wrap directly over this bone. Tighten it until the weight of the pack rests 80% on your pelvis, not your shoulders.

Step 2: Shoulder Straps (The Stabilizers)

Pull down and back on the shoulder strap tails. They should wrap snugly around your shoulders with no gaps, but they shouldn't be carrying the primary weight. If your shoulders are aching, your hip belt is too loose.

Step 3: Load Lifters (The 45-Degree Rule)

These are the small straps on top of your shoulders. Pull them forward to snug the top of the pack closer to your back. Ideally, these straps should form a 45-degree angle with the top of the pack frame.

Step 4: The Sternum Strap (The Finisher)

Buckle the chest strap and tighten it until your arms can move freely. This pulls the shoulder straps inward and away from your armpits, preventing chafing during long treks.

Final Check

Walk around. Jump slightly. The pack should move with you, not against you. If it sways, tighten the load lifters. If it pinches, loosen the sternum strap.
